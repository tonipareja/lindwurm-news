The first month of a Warcrow campaign should feel less like a ladder and more like a journey across contested ground. Players need a reason to care about each battle beyond the immediate win or loss, and Lindwurm gives us plenty of hooks: patrols disappearing in the mist, border forts sending broken messages, rival commanders claiming the same road, and small warbands trying to turn a local rumor into strategic advantage. A strong campaign opening does not need a heavy rules packet. It needs a clean premise, a clear map, and enough room for players to add their own grudges.

At the table, that means starting with scenarios that ask simple questions. Who controls the pass? Which side recovers the courier? Can a wounded unit hold long enough for reinforcements to arrive? These are easy to understand before dice are rolled, but they leave plenty of space for memorable turns. When a player loses a model to a desperate countercharge, or when an objective changes hands at the last moment, the result should become part of the next week's story rather than a line in a spreadsheet.

![A dark battleline advancing through a valley](/images/warcrow-battleline.svg)

The first week can be built around scouting. Keep the games small and focused, with objectives placed away from the center so movement matters. This lets faster units shine without making raw damage the only path to victory. If a faction has tools for control, positioning, or denial, this is where those tools should be visible. The goal is not to decide the campaign in the opening game. The goal is to introduce the geography and show players what the contested region feels like.

The second week can escalate into supply lines. Once players know the terrain, they should start fighting over the things that make armies move: food, maps, ammunition, safe roads, and local guides. A mission built around supply does not have to be complicated. Give each side two markers to protect and one neutral cache to fight over. At the end, the winner might choose a small advantage for the following round, such as redeploying one unit after setup or forcing the opponent to reveal a hidden objective first.

The trick is to keep rewards interesting without making them oppressive. Campaign bonuses should feel like texture, not like a mathematical avalanche. A player who wins week one should feel rewarded, but a player who loses week one should still want to come back. Small narrative privileges work well here. Let winners name a landmark, decide where the next battle happens, or claim a rumor that becomes true. Those rewards make the campaign feel alive while avoiding a runaway leader problem.

![A campaign map with routes and objectives](/images/warcrow-campaign-map.svg)

By the third week, the campaign can reveal its first real complication. Perhaps the road was not empty. Perhaps the recovered map points to an old settlement that neither side expected to find. Perhaps a neutral force, local militia, or wandering monster changes the assumptions of the conflict. The important thing is that the players should feel the campaign responding to them. Their earlier results should matter, but not in a way that locks the story into a single predictable path.

This is also a good moment to bring hobby progress into the structure. A Warcrow news site can help here by publishing weekly painting prompts alongside battle reports. One week could ask for a finished leader. Another could focus on bases, shields, or a single unit. These prompts give slower painters a manageable target, and they help the site become more than a place for announcements. It becomes a record of the local community's version of Lindwurm.

Rules previews can sit naturally inside that rhythm. Instead of dropping a full mechanical analysis in isolation, a post can ask how a new profile changes the current campaign. Does a fresh character help break defensive lines? Does a new unit give one faction a better answer to mobile objectives? Does a rules tweak make a forgotten option worth testing again? Framing rules this way makes the article useful for players who are already thinking about their next game.

![An open rulebook with unit cards and tokens](/images/warcrow-rules-spread.svg)

The fourth week should feel like the campaign is narrowing toward a decision. By now, players have names for places, grudges between units, and a sense of which commanders are reliable. This is the moment to introduce linked objectives. If one side secured supplies earlier, perhaps they can choose the time of day. If another recovered the map, perhaps they know where a hidden route begins. These details do not need to be large, but they should remind everyone that previous games left marks on the world.

For the website layout, a long article like this is a useful stress test because it asks the design to do more than display a title and a card. The body needs comfortable line length, enough contrast for extended reading, and images that break the rhythm without feeling like advertisements. If the home page shows every post in full, then spacing becomes especially important. Each article needs to end clearly before the next one begins, or the feed becomes tiring after only a few minutes.

The archive page serves a different purpose. It should remain fast and scannable, because sometimes readers only want to find the last rules note or painting guide. The search icon in the header helps bridge both modes. A reader can browse the continuous home page when they want to catch up, or use the archive and search when they remember a specific unit, faction, or scenario. That split keeps the site simple without forcing every visit into the same pattern.

At the end of the first month, the campaign should produce a recap article. This is where the news format becomes powerful. Gather the winning moments, the painted models, the most dramatic failures, and the questions for month two. A recap gives closure without ending the story. It also gives new readers a clean entry point: they can understand what happened, see the tone of the community, and decide whether they want to join the next round.

The best version of this kind of site will not try to imitate an official announcement feed too closely. It should feel like a field journal: quick when news breaks, useful when rules need explaining, and generous when the community has something worth showing. Warcrow has enough texture to support that tone. A simple site, updated consistently, can make even small releases feel connected to a larger campaign.
